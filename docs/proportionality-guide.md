# Proportionality: How Much of DORA Applies to You

DORA does not treat every firm the same. The rules scale to your size, risk, and complexity. This guide helps you work out how much applies to your firm. It does not replace legal advice, and it does not exempt you from the core duties. Confirm the exact categories against the current DORA text and your regulator's guidance.

---

## 1. The plain-English idea

Every in-scope firm must manage ICT risk. What changes with proportionality is the depth and formality, not the goal. A large, complex firm needs detailed, tested, measured controls. A small, simple firm needs the same protections but can run them in a lighter way.

Two separate questions decide your position:

1. **Are you in scope at all?** (Almost all financial entities are.)
2. **Can you use the simplified framework (Art. 16)?** (Only some smaller or lower-risk firms can.)

There is also a third question that is not about size: **which of your functions are "critical or important"?** The strongest rules attach to those functions no matter how big you are.

## 2. Step-by-step decision guide

Work through these in order and write down your answer and reason for each. That written record is itself evidence a regulator or auditor will want to see.

### Step 1: Are you a financial entity in scope?

If your firm is any of the following, you are in scope: bank or credit institution, payment institution, e-money institution, account information service provider, investment firm, crypto-asset service provider, central securities depository, central counterparty, trading venue, trade repository, manager of alternative investment funds, management company, data reporting service provider, insurance or reinsurance undertaking, insurance intermediary, institution for occupational retirement provision, credit rating agency, administrator of critical benchmarks, crowdfunding service provider, or securitisation repository.

- If **yes**: continue to Step 2.
- If you are unsure or think you are not listed: get legal confirmation before assuming you are out of scope.

### Step 2: Are you excluded or exempt?

DORA lists a small number of exclusions (for example, certain very small or specific entity types named in Art. 2). These are narrow.

- If you are clearly named as excluded: record the basis and stop. DORA does not apply.
- Otherwise: continue to Step 3.

### Step 3: Do you qualify for the simplified framework (Art. 16)?

Art. 16 lets certain firms use a lighter ICT risk management framework. The kinds of firms this is aimed at include:

- Small and non-interconnected investment firms.
- Certain small payment institutions and e-money institutions.
- Small institutions for occupational retirement provision.
- Certain other smaller entities named in Art. 16(1).

Ask:

- Does your firm's legal type appear in the Art. 16(1) list?
- Do you meet the size and interconnectedness conditions for that type?

- If **yes to both**: you may use the simplified framework. Go to Section 3.
- If **no**: you must apply the full framework. Go to Section 4.
- If **unsure**: treat yourself as full-framework until you have written legal confirmation. It is safer to over-comply than to guess wrong.

### Step 4: Which of your functions are critical or important?

Do this regardless of your answers above. List your business functions and mark each one "critical or important" if a failure would seriously harm your firm, your clients, or the market. The strongest requirements (for example, enhanced contract terms, exit testing, and TLPT if you are named) attach to these functions.

### Step 5: Are you named for TLPT?

Advanced threat-led testing (Art. 26) applies only to firms the regulator specifically identifies, based on their importance and risk. You do not opt in.

- If your regulator has named you: the TLPT rules apply. Use the TLPT scoping worksheet.
- If not: you still do baseline testing, but not TLPT.

## 3. What the simplified framework still requires

Being eligible for the simplified framework does not mean "do much less of everything". You still must have, in a lighter but real form:

- A documented ICT risk management framework, owned and approved internally.
- Continuous monitoring of the security and functioning of your ICT systems.
- Controls that reduce the impact of ICT risk.
- A way to detect unusual activity and incidents.
- Business continuity and recovery arrangements.
- Periodic testing of those arrangements.
- Incident management and the duty to report major incidents.

In short: the simplified framework reduces formality and detail. It does not remove the duty to protect, detect, recover, and report.

## 4. What the full framework adds

If you are not eligible for the simplified framework, you apply the full set across all five pillars at full depth: the complete ICT risk framework (Art. 5 to 16), full incident management and reporting (Art. 17 to 23), the full testing programme (Art. 24 to 27, including TLPT if named), full third-party rules (Art. 28 to 44), and information sharing if you choose (Art. 45).

## 6. Common mistakes

- Assuming "small firm" means "DORA does not really apply". It does. It just applies more lightly.
- Guessing simplified-framework eligibility instead of confirming it. If wrong, you under-comply.
- Forgetting that critical-or-important-function rules apply even to small firms.
- Not writing down the reasoning. Assessors want to see the "why", not just the "what".
