# Pillar 0: Governance, Organisation and Proportionality (Playbook)

DORA articles: Art. 4 (proportionality), Art. 5 (governance), Art. 6 (framework).

Read first: Glossary and Proportionality guide.

This is a playbook. It gives you the steps, the order, the owner for each step, and ready-to-copy artifacts so you can set up DORA governance from nothing. Style note: plain English, no em dashes. Copy any table into your own documents and edit it.

## At a glance

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| Goal | Make the board truly own ICT risk, with clear roles, real oversight, money, and skill. |
| Who leads | Company secretary or Chief Risk Officer, sponsored by a named board member. |
| Typical time | 8 to 10 weeks to stand up; then ongoing. |
| Main outputs | Approved framework, RACI, board reporting pack, proportionality assessment, training plan, budget line. |
| Done when | The board has approved the framework in minutes, one owner is accountable, and reporting runs on a schedule. |

## 1. What this pillar is really about

DORA puts the board in charge of ICT risk and does not let them pass the buck. The board cannot say "that is the IT team's problem". They own it. This pillar makes that ownership real: clear roles, real oversight, enough money and skill, and a sensible view of how much of DORA applies to you.

Get this pillar right and the other four become much easier, because someone senior is accountable and the operating model is clear. Get it wrong and everything else drifts.

## 2. Before you start (prerequisites)

- A named executive sponsor (for example the CRO or COO).
- Access to the board or its risk committee to get items on the agenda.
- A rough list of your business functions (you will refine this in Pillar 1).
- The Proportionality guide, read once.

## 3. Step-by-step build

Each step lists who does it and what you should have at the end.

Step 1: Confirm scope and proportionality. Owner: CRO. Work through the Proportionality guide. Write one page that states: are you in scope, do you use the full or simplified framework, and why. Output: a signed proportionality assessment.

Step 2: Name the accountable person. Owner: Board. Name one board member or one committee as accountable for ICT risk. Record it in the board minutes and in the terms of reference. Output: a minute naming the owner.

Step 3: Define the operating model. Owner: CRO. Set up the three lines of defence and a RACI (see the sample below). Make sure every key activity has exactly one accountable owner. Output: an approved RACI and a short operating-model note.

Step 4: Set up board reporting. Owner: CRO with the CISO. Decide what the board sees, how often, and in what format. Use the reporting pack outline below. Output: a reporting calendar and a pack template.

Step 5: Agree the budget and training. Owner: Sponsor. Get a named resilience budget line and a board training plan approved. Output: a budget approval and a training plan with dates.

Step 6: Approve the framework. Owner: Board. Once Pillar 1 has drafted the framework, the board formally approves it, with real discussion recorded. Output: dated approval minutes.

Step 7: Set the review rhythm. Owner: Company secretary. Put the yearly framework review, the reporting cycle, and internal audit coverage of ICT into the governance calendar. Output: a recurring calendar.

## 4. Ready-to-use artifact: board ICT risk charter (outline to copy)

Copy this into a one-page charter and fill the brackets.

- Purpose: to oversee ICT and operational resilience risk under DORA.
- Accountable owner: [board member or committee name].
- Duties: approve the ICT risk framework; approve the continuity policy and recovery plans; approve the third-party policy; approve the internal audit plan for ICT; set and review the resilience budget; complete training; ensure roles are clear.
- Meeting rhythm: [monthly or quarterly].
- Standing inputs: the board reporting pack (see below).
- Decisions reserved to the board: risk appetite, major spend, accepting or rejecting key risks.
- Review: this charter is reviewed yearly.

## 5. Ready-to-use artifact: RACI

R = does the work, A = accountable (only one), C = consulted, I = informed.

| Activity | Board | CISO | IT Ops | Risk | Internal Audit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Approve ICT risk framework | A | R | C | C | I |
| Run day-to-day security controls | I | A | R | C | I |
| Classify and report major incidents | A | R | C | C | I |
| Oversee third-party risk | A | C | C | R | I |
| Run resilience testing | I | A | R | C | C |
| Independently test the controls | I | I | I | C | A/R |
| Approve the resilience budget | A | C | C | R | I |

Adjust the roles to your structure. The rule is simple: every row has exactly one A.

## 6. Ready-to-use artifact: board reporting pack (one to two pages)

Put these on every pack so the board can challenge and decide:

- Top ICT risks and any change since last time (a simple red, amber, green view works well).
- Major incidents in the period and their status.
- Results of any resilience testing.
- Third-party risk and concentration highlights.
- Remediation actions, with owners and due dates.
- Legal-breach flags, called out clearly and separately.
- Decisions the board needs to make now.

## 7. Proportionality in one page (Art. 4 and 16)

DORA scales to your firm. It changes the depth, not the goal. Use the Proportionality guide to answer three questions and write down the answers:

- Are you in scope? (Almost all financial entities are.)
- Can you use the simplified framework (Art. 16)? (Only some smaller or lower-risk firms can.)
- Which of your functions are critical or important? (The strongest rules attach to these, no matter your size.)

If unsure about the simplified framework, treat yourself as full-framework until a lawyer confirms otherwise. It is safer to over-comply than to guess wrong.

## 8. Control objectives

| ID | Control objective |
|---|---|
| GOV-01 | The board has approved the ICT risk framework and reviews it at least yearly. |
| GOV-02 | Roles, responsibilities, and accountability for ICT risk are documented and shared. |
| GOV-03 | The board gets regular ICT risk reporting and shows real challenge. |
| GOV-04 | Board members complete ICT resilience training suited to their role. |
| GOV-05 | An adequate resilience budget is set and reviewed. |
| GOV-06 | The proportionality tier and simplified-framework eligibility are assessed and written down. |
| GOV-07 | A three-lines-of-defence model is defined and internal audit covers ICT independently. |

## 9. Acceptance checklist (done when)

- [ ] A signed proportionality assessment exists and states full or simplified, with reasons.
- [ ] One board member or committee is named accountable in the minutes.
- [ ] A RACI is approved and every key activity has one accountable owner.
- [ ] A board reporting pack template and calendar exist, and the first pack has been delivered.
- [ ] A resilience budget line is approved and a board training plan has dates.
- [ ] The board has approved the framework, with real discussion recorded.
- [ ] The yearly review, reporting cycle, and internal audit of ICT are in the calendar.

## 10. Evidence to keep

Board minutes approving the framework and key policies (dated), the org chart and RACI, the terms of reference for any ICT risk committee, the proportionality assessment, board training records, budget approvals that mention resilience, and the internal audit plan and reports covering ICT.

## 11. Common mistakes

- Treating ICT risk as only an IT matter, not a board duty.
- No written proportionality reasoning. Assessors want the "why".
- Board reporting that lists numbers but shows no challenge or decisions.
- Assuming the simplified framework removes testing and continuity. It does not.

## 12. Quick self-check

- Can you point to board minutes from this year approving the framework, with real discussion? If not, fix this first.
- Is there exactly one person or committee accountable for ICT risk? If ownership is fuzzy, everything downstream will be too.
- Have you written down whether you use the full or simplified framework, and why? If not, do the proportionality assessment now.
