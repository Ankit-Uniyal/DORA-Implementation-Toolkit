# Glossary of DORA Terms

Plain-English meanings of the terms used across this toolkit. If you are new to DORA, read this first. Where a term has a strict legal meaning, this glossary keeps the plain meaning and points you to the article.

---

**DORA.** The Digital Operational Resilience Act, Regulation (EU) 2022/2554. It is an EU law that makes financial firms manage the risk of their technology failing or being attacked. It became law on 16 January 2023 and applied from 17 January 2025.

**Financial entity.** Any firm that DORA applies to. This is a wide list: banks, payment firms, e-money firms, investment firms, crypto firms, insurers, and more. If you are one of these, DORA applies to you.

**Competent authority.** The regulator that supervises your firm in your country (for example, a central bank or a financial regulator). This is who you report major incidents to.

**ESAs.** The three European Supervisory Authorities: the EBA (banking), EIOPA (insurance and pensions), and ESMA (markets). They write the detailed rules that sit under DORA.

**Level 1 text.** The main DORA regulation itself. It sets the high-level rules.

**RTS (Regulatory Technical Standards).** Detailed rules written by the ESAs that fill in the specifics of DORA. They are legally binding. Example: the RTS that sets the exact thresholds for when an incident counts as "major".

**ITS (Implementing Technical Standards).** Rules from the ESAs that set out standard forms, templates, and formats. Example: the ITS that gives the template for reporting an incident.

> Simple way to remember it: DORA says "you must report major incidents". The RTS says "here is exactly what "major" means". The ITS says "here is the exact form to use".

**ICT.** Information and Communication Technology. In plain terms: your IT systems, software, networks, and data.

**ICT risk.** The chance that your technology fails, is attacked, or otherwise causes harm to your business or your customers.

**Critical or important function.** A business activity where a failure would seriously harm your firm, your customers, or the financial system. DORA gives these functions the strongest requirements. Deciding which of your functions are "critical or important" is one of the first and most important steps.

**Information asset.** A collection of information that has value, whether stored digitally or not (for example, a customer database).

**ICT asset.** A piece of software or hardware in your technology estate (for example, an application, a server, or a network device).

**Management body.** The board or senior leadership. DORA makes them personally accountable for ICT risk. They cannot pass this responsibility down and forget about it.

**Proportionality.** The idea that the rules apply in a way that matches your firm's size, risk, and complexity. A large bank does much more than a small firm. It does not mean small firms are exempt.

**Simplified ICT risk management framework.** A lighter set of rules (Art. 16) that some smaller or lower-risk firms can use instead of the full framework. See the proportionality guide to check if you qualify.

**RTO (Recovery Time Objective).** The maximum time a system can be down before it causes unacceptable harm. Example: "core payments must be back within 2 hours".

**RPO (Recovery Point Objective).** The maximum amount of data you can afford to lose, measured in time. Example: "we can lose at most 15 minutes of data".

**BC/DR.** Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery. Your plans and capabilities to keep running, or recover quickly, when something goes wrong.

**Incident.** An event that disrupts your ICT services or reduces their security.

**Major incident.** An incident serious enough that you must report it to your regulator. Whether an incident is "major" is decided using the criteria in Art. 18 and the thresholds in the RTS.

**Significant cyber threat.** A serious cyber threat that has not yet caused an incident. You can choose to tell your regulator about it, but you do not have to.

**Resilience testing.** Checking that your systems and processes actually hold up. This ranges from basic scans and penetration tests to advanced threat-led testing.

**TLPT (Threat-Led Penetration Testing).** Advanced testing where skilled testers act like real attackers, using real threat intelligence, against your live systems. Only firms that the regulator names have to do it. See Art. 26.

**TIBER-EU.** A European framework for running threat-led red team tests. The DORA rules for TLPT are built to line up with it.

**Red team.** The group of testers who play the role of the attacker in an advanced test.

**Control team.** The small internal group that safely manages a TLPT so it does not harm live services.

**ICT third-party service provider.** An outside company that provides you with an ICT service (for example, a cloud provider or a software vendor).

**CTPP (Critical ICT Third-Party Provider).** A third-party provider that the ESAs judge to be so important to the financial system that they oversee it directly at the EU level.

**Register of information.** A structured record of all your contracts for ICT services. You must keep it up to date and report it to your regulator at least once a year. See Art. 28.

**Concentration risk.** The risk of relying too heavily on one provider, so that if that one provider fails, many of your important functions fail at once.

**Sub-outsourcing.** When your provider passes part of the work to another provider further down the chain. This adds risk that you still have to manage.

**Exit strategy.** Your plan for moving away from a provider without disruption, if you have to. For important functions this must be written down and tested.

**Information sharing.** Firms sharing cyber threat information with each other to help everyone defend better. This is voluntary. See Art. 45.

**Three lines of defence.** A common way to organise risk management. First line: the people who own and run the controls day to day. Second line: risk and compliance functions that oversee them. Third line: internal audit, which independently checks that it all works.

**Maturity level.** In this toolkit, a score from 0 to 5 that describes how well a control is set up and running. See the maturity model.

**Gap.** A place where you do not yet meet a DORA requirement, or do not meet it well enough.
