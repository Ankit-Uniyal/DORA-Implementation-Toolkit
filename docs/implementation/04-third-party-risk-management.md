# Pillar 4 — ICT Third-Party Risk Management

**DORA articles:** Art. 28 (general principles), Art. 29 (preliminary assessment of ICT concentration risk), Art. 30 (key contractual provisions), Art. 31–44 (Oversight Framework for critical ICT third-party providers — CTPPs). Supplemented by the RTS/ITS on the register of information, RTS on subcontracting, and RTS/ITS on the policy for ICT services supporting critical or important functions.

---

## 1. Purpose

ICT third-party risk is treated as an integral part of ICT risk. DORA imposes obligations on financial entities managing their ICT supply chain **and** creates a pan-EU **Oversight Framework** under which the ESAs designate and directly oversee **critical ICT third-party service providers (CTPPs)**.

## 2. General principles (Art. 28)

- Manage ICT third-party risk as part of the ICT risk framework, in line with the proportionality principle.
- Adopt and regularly review a **strategy on ICT third-party risk**, including a policy on the use of ICT services supporting critical or important functions.
- Maintain and update a **register of information** on all contractual arrangements for the use of ICT services, distinguishing those supporting critical/important functions. Report at least yearly to the competent authority on new arrangements, categories, and the CTPPs used; inform the authority in a timely manner about planned arrangements supporting critical/important functions.
- Before entering into a contract, perform due diligence, assess whether the arrangement covers a critical/important function, assess supervisory conditions, and identify/assess conflicts of interest.
- Consider **concentration risk** and the risks of sub-outsourcing chains.
- Ensure ability to exit arrangements without disruption, with documented **exit strategies** and transition plans for critical/important functions.

## 3. Preliminary assessment of concentration risk (Art. 29)

When assessing arrangements supporting critical/important functions, evaluate: whether the provider is not easily substitutable; the implications of multiple arrangements with the same provider or closely connected providers; and the risks of sub-outsourcing, especially where the sub-contractor is established in a third country.

## 4. Key contractual provisions (Art. 30)

Contracts must be documented in writing. **All** ICT contracts must include, among others: description of services; locations where services are provided and data processed (with notification of changes); data protection provisions and access, recovery and return of data on insolvency/termination; service level descriptions; the provider's obligation to assist at no/predetermined cost on an incident; cooperation with authorities; termination rights; and participation in the entity's security awareness/training as relevant.

For arrangements **supporting critical or important functions**, contracts must additionally include: full service level descriptions with precise quantitative/qualitative performance targets; notice periods and reporting obligations; requirements to implement contingency plans and ICT security measures; participation in and full cooperation with the entity's **TLPT**; unrestricted **rights of access, inspection and audit** (by the entity, appointed third parties, and competent authorities); clear termination rights and associated exit strategies with a mandatory transition period.

## 5. Oversight Framework for CTPPs (Art. 31–44)

The ESAs designate ICT third-party providers as **critical** based on criteria such as systemic impact, importance of the financial entities relying on them, substitutability, and interconnectedness. Each CTPP is assigned a **Lead Overseer** (one of the ESAs) with powers to request information, conduct investigations and inspections, issue recommendations, and impose periodic penalty payments. Financial entities must **take into account** whether their providers are designated CTPPs and consider the associated risks; where a CTPP does not follow oversight recommendations, entities may be required to temporarily suspend or terminate the relevant services.

## 6. Control objectives

| ID | Control objective |
|----|-------------------|
| TPP-01 | A board-approved ICT third-party strategy and policy on services supporting critical/important functions exists. |
| TPP-02 | A complete register of information covers all ICT contractual arrangements and flags critical/important functions. |
| TPP-03 | Pre-contract due diligence assesses risk, criticality, concentration and conflicts of interest. |
| TPP-04 | Contracts contain all mandatory provisions; enhanced provisions apply to critical/important functions. |
| TPP-05 | Audit, access and inspection rights are secured and exercised on a risk basis. |
| TPP-06 | Concentration and sub-outsourcing risks are assessed and monitored. |
| TPP-07 | Documented, tested exit strategies exist for critical/important functions. |
| TPP-08 | CTPP designations are tracked and reflected in risk management. |
| TPP-09 | The register is reported to the competent authority at least annually. |

## 7. Implementation steps

1. **Approve the strategy & policy** for ICT third-party risk and for services supporting critical/important functions.
2. **Build the register of information** aligned to the ITS format (see the third-party register template); classify each arrangement by criticality.
3. **Stand up pre-contract due diligence** covering risk, criticality, supervisory conditions, concentration and conflicts of interest.
4. **Remediate contracts**: gap-check existing contracts against Art. 30 mandatory and enhanced clauses; renegotiate as needed.
5. **Operationalise oversight**: monitoring of SLAs, incidents, audit rights, sub-outsourcing changes, and CTPP designations.
6. **Develop exit strategies** and transition plans for each critical/important function; test their feasibility.
7. **Report** the register to the competent authority annually and notify planned critical/important arrangements.

## 8. Evidence to retain

- Approved third-party strategy and policy.
- Register of information (current and submitted versions).
- Due-diligence assessments and criticality determinations.
- Contract clause gap analyses and executed contracts with mandatory/enhanced provisions.
- Concentration-risk and sub-outsourcing assessments.
- Exit strategy documents and test evidence.
- Records of annual regulatory reporting of the register.

## 9. Common pitfalls

- Incomplete register (missing intra-group arrangements, sub-outsourced services, or SaaS/cloud not treated as ICT services).
- Legacy contracts lacking mandatory clauses, especially audit/access rights and TLPT cooperation.
- Exit strategies that exist on paper but are untested and operationally infeasible.
- Ignoring concentration where many critical functions rely on a single non-substitutable provider.
